The American Death Triangle is the classic example of a dangerously unsafe climbing anchor. It is incredibly easy to build, but also incredibly easy to avoid.

The reason the Death Triangle is such a bad idea is Freshman Physics, and is easily understood using Force-Vector Diagrams.

The V Anchor

First, let's look at a better anchor, the classic "V" anchor.

F =
W
2 * cos(Θ/2)

The Death Triangle

Now let's look at the Death Triangle.

F =
W
2 * sin(π/4 - Θ/4)

The Comparison

What we see above is that the extra side on the Death Triangle anchor creates a much great force on the climbing bolts. This means a much greater force on the sling/webbing that the anchor is built from. These extra forces can pluck protection out of the wall and snap cords. And the discussion above works the same for anchors with more than two pieces of protection.

The explanation above uses static forces and static loads. That is, the math/physics is simpler because nothing is moving. In reality, the most dangerous situation is when a climber falls, and for a short time they exert a much greater force on the anchor than just their resting weight.
